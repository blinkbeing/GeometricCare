The hospital bed has not changed its geometry since the medieval ward. Rectangular rows designed for cruciform halls, not for human bodies. The form predates germ theory, predates the stethoscope, predates every modern understanding of physiology and care. Yet this geometry still dictates how hospitals are built, how departments are organized, how data is trapped, and how patients fall.
Design comes first. Process follows. Departments form around the process. Data gets trapped in departments. Interoperability becomes an industry. Burnout clinicians spend their nights documenting care they already delivered. Patients fall from beds designed for wards, not bodies. Costs get capped. Pensions get cut. The best nurses leave.
Healthcare costs skyrocket. Four percent of humans outspending the next ten nations combined. Projected $9 trillion by 2034. All poured into a geometry that was never designed to heal—while politicians debate incremental reforms on a structure never built for the purpose. The reaching hand finds only rail and void.
Geometric Care is an open hardware and software initiative to replace the rectangular bed with an intelligent hexagonal platform. The hexagon restores 10-point clinical access, enabling eyeball-to-eyeball medicine without the obstruction of rails and corners. AI-driven fall-risk assessment scores CNS, CVS, skeletal, GI, urology, and respiratory systems in real time. Auto-documentation eliminates the pajama-time burden, returning hours to clinicians. Universal interoperability specifications break the silos before they form. The platform is designed for universal adoption: open standards, open protocols, and hardware that can be fabricated at cost in local workshops.
This repository contains proof-of-concept hardware designs, assessment frameworks, PDSA quality improvement protocols, and interoperability specifications. The goal is not a better bed. The goal is a new geometry of care—one that recognizes the perceptual boundary where patients blink, fall, recover, and connect.
We are building the first units at cost in local workshops. If you engineer hardware, train models, write protocols, or simply believe the reaching hand deserves more than rail and void, contribute.
